    Bank deposits and post-office savings schemes remain the backbone of conservative, risk-free savings in India. But the three most common instruments — FD, RD, and PPF — compound very differently, and getting that mechanic right matters for an accurate maturity estimate.

    Fixed Deposit (FD)

    An FD is a lump-sum deposit for a fixed tenure at a fixed rate, most commonly compounded quarterly by Indian banks. FD interest is fully taxable at your income slab rate, and banks deduct TDS once interest crosses ₹40,000/year (₹50,000 for senior citizens) in a financial year.

    Recurring Deposit (RD)

    An RD is the "SIP of fixed income" — a fixed monthly deposit that compounds over the tenure. It builds the same monthly-savings discipline as a SIP, but with a guaranteed, market-independent return, making it well suited to short, certain-timeline goals rather than long-term growth.

    Public Provident Fund (PPF)

    PPF is a government-backed, long-term scheme with EEE (Exempt-Exempt-Exempt) tax status — your contribution, the interest earned, and the final maturity amount are all tax-free. The rate is reviewed by the Ministry of Finance every quarter, and interest is calculated monthly on the lowest balance between the 5th and last day of the month but credited (and compounded) annually.

    Frequently asked questions

    Which is better — FD or PPF?

    They serve different purposes. FDs are flexible with tenures from as little as 7 days up to 10 years and are fully taxable; PPF locks your money for 15 years but is completely tax-free and typically pays a comparable or better post-tax return for someone in a higher tax bracket. Many savers use both.

    Can I withdraw from PPF before 15 years?

    Partial withdrawals are allowed from the 7th year onward under specific conditions, and a loan against the balance is available between the 3rd and 6th year — but PPF is designed as a long-term, mostly untouched holding.

    Is RD interest the same as FD interest for the same rate?

    No — because an RD's monthly deposits each compound for a different length of time (the first deposit compounds the longest, the last barely at all), the effective yield on an RD is lower than an FD of the same headline rate and tenure, holding the same total amount invested.
