    An EMI (Equated Monthly Installment) is the fixed monthly payment on a loan, combining both principal and interest. Early in a loan's life, most of each EMI goes toward interest; only later does the principal portion start dominating — which is exactly why prepaying a loan early saves disproportionately more interest than prepaying the same amount later.

    How EMI is calculated

    This calculator uses the standard reducing-balance EMI formula used by Indian banks and NBFCs:

    EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / ((1+r)^n − 1)

    Where P is the loan amount, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n is the total number of monthly installments.

    Home loan

    Home loans carry the lowest rates among common retail loans, since the property itself secures the loan. Principal repayment qualifies for a deduction under Section 80C (up to ₹1.5 lakh/year) and interest under Section 24(b) (up to ₹2 lakh/year for a self-occupied property) — both of which meaningfully lower the real, post-tax cost, and are reflected in this calculator's tips.

    Personal loan

    Personal loans need no collateral, which is why rates run considerably higher than secured loans. There is no tax benefit in the typical case, so treat the full EMI as a real cost, and keep the tenure as short as you can afford.

    Education loan

    Education loans usually include a moratorium period — repayment (or at least full EMI repayment) typically starts 6–12 months after course completion, not immediately. The full interest paid is deductible without an upper cap under Section 80E for 8 years from when repayment begins, a materially better tax treatment than a personal loan.

    Car & bike loan

    Vehicles depreciate 15–20% a year, so a loan tenure that outlasts the vehicle's useful resale value works against you. Keeping the tenure short — generally 5 years or less for a car, 3 years or less for a bike — avoids owing more than the vehicle is worth for longer than necessary.

    Frequently asked questions

    What is a good FOIR (EMI-to-income ratio)?

    Indian lenders commonly look for total EMIs (across all loans) to stay under roughly 40–50% of monthly income, often called the Fixed Obligation to Income Ratio (FOIR). This calculator includes an optional income field that checks your EMI against that guideline.

    Does prepaying a loan early actually save much?

    Yes, disproportionately so — because interest is charged on the reducing balance, an early prepayment removes principal while more months of interest were still ahead of it, saving more than the same prepayment made later in the loan's life. The amortization table in this calculator shows exactly how the principal/interest split shifts over time.

    Is loan interest always simple to compare across lenders?

    Not from the headline rate alone — always compare the effective annual rate including processing fees and any bundled insurance, not just the advertised interest rate, since two loans with the same rate can have meaningfully different real costs.
