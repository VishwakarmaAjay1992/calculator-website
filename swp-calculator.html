A Systematic Withdrawal Plan (SWP) is the mirror image of a SIP: instead of investing a fixed amount every month, you withdraw one, while the rest of your corpus stays invested and continues to grow (or shrink, if withdrawals outpace returns).

How this SWP calculator works

Each month, the calculator applies your expected monthly return to the remaining balance, then subtracts your withdrawal — compounding the growth on whatever's left, exactly the way an actual SWP from a mutual fund works. If your withdrawal exceeds what the corpus can sustain, the calculator flags the exact month your corpus is projected to run out.

The core trade-off: withdrawal rate vs. corpus life

The single biggest lever in an SWP is your withdrawal rate — the percentage of your corpus you draw out each year. A withdrawal rate comfortably below your expected return lets the corpus sustain itself indefinitely or even grow; a rate above it guarantees depletion, just a question of when. This calculator makes that math visible instead of leaving it as a guess.

SWP vs. simply spending savings

Keeping a retirement or income corpus invested via an SWP — rather than parking it entirely in a low-yield savings account and drawing down — lets the invested portion continue compounding while you withdraw only what you need each month. This is one reason SWPs are commonly used for retirement income planning in India, often from equity or hybrid mutual funds, though the mix should reflect your risk tolerance and how soon you need the money.

Frequently asked questions

Is SWP income taxable?

Withdrawals from a mutual fund SWP are treated as redemptions, so the gain portion (not the whole withdrawal) is subject to capital gains tax, at rates that depend on the fund type and holding period. This is generally more tax-efficient than fully taxable interest income, but you should confirm current rules with a tax advisor since capital gains tax treatment can change.

What withdrawal rate is considered safe?

There is no universal number, but many planners treat an annual withdrawal rate in the range of 4–6% of the corpus, adjusted for your specific return assumptions and time horizon, as a starting point for testing sustainability — this calculator lets you test your own assumptions directly rather than relying on a rule of thumb.

Can I combine SIP and SWP in one plan?

Yes — this is a common pattern: run a SIP during your working years to build the corpus, then switch to an SWP after retirement to draw an income from it. The Retirement calculator on this site models exactly that combined journey in one place.
