Practical suggestions — Retirement Planning

  • This assumes your monthly withdrawal grows with inflation every year in retirement, so your purchasing power stays constant — a more realistic (and more demanding) target than a flat withdrawal.
  • Spread the required SIP across EPF, NPS, PPF, and equity mutual funds rather than one instrument — NPS also gives an extra ₹50,000 deduction under Section 80CCD(1B) on top of the ₹1.5L Section 80C limit.
  • Revisit this plan every 2–3 years. Income, expenses, and market conditions change — a retirement plan is a living document, not a one-time calculation.
  • If the required SIP looks out of reach today, a later retirement age or a higher equity allocation early on (when you can absorb volatility) usually closes the gap faster than cutting today's expenses.

Most retirement "rules of thumb" (like "save 25x your annual expense") ignore two things that matter enormously: inflation between now and retirement, and inflation during retirement itself, since your expenses keep rising even after you stop earning.

How this calculator is different

Rather than using a simplified formula, this tool actually simulates your retirement month by month: your monthly expense grows with inflation every year you're retired, while your remaining corpus earns your assumed post-retirement return. It then works backward — via search rather than a rough approximation — to find the minimum corpus that doesn't run out before your specified life expectancy, accounting for any existing retirement savings you already have.

The two numbers that matter most

1. Corpus needed at retirement

This is the amount you need on the day you retire so that inflation-adjusted monthly withdrawals, growing every year, don't outlast your expected lifespan.

2. Required monthly SIP

Working from today's date to your retirement age, this is the monthly investment — assumed to grow at your specified pre-retirement return — needed to close the gap between your existing savings and the corpus required.

Why the return assumption split matters

Pre-retirement, most people can afford more equity exposure and a higher assumed return, since they have years to ride out volatility. Post-retirement, a more conservative, income-oriented allocation is common, since a market downturn early in retirement can permanently damage a corpus that's simultaneously being drawn down — a risk known as "sequence of returns risk." This calculator deliberately asks for both return rates separately rather than one blended number.

Frequently asked questions

What inflation rate should I assume for retirement planning?

India's long-term consumer inflation has broadly averaged in the range of 5–7% over extended periods, though it varies year to year. Many planners use 6% as a starting assumption, but it is worth testing your plan at a higher rate too, since underestimating inflation is the most common retirement-planning mistake.

Should NPS be part of my retirement plan?

The National Pension System (NPS) offers an additional tax deduction of ₹50,000 under Section 80CCD(1B), on top of the ₹1.5 lakh Section 80C limit, making it a common complement to EPF, PPF, and equity SIPs in a retirement portfolio — though it comes with its own withdrawal and annuitization rules worth understanding separately.

What if the required monthly SIP looks unaffordable?

A later retirement age, a higher pre-retirement return (via more equity allocation while you have time to absorb volatility), or a lower post-retirement expense target are usually more effective levers than simply lowering today's spending — try adjusting the retirement age slider to see the effect.
